Task Instructions for Section 1 (Click to expand)

In Section 1, You will be given a context sentence and ten topics generated by a machine. We want to know whether the ten topics are useful in terms of writing the continuation of the context sentence.

Your job is to judge

  • a) which topics do NOT promote the narrative (i.e., the topics that do not provide additional information if being observed in the continuation), and
  • b) which topics are NOT very likely to appear in reasonable continuations (i.e., it is hard to imagine a reasonable continuation that mentions the topic or the topic does not have a clear meaning)?

    • Each option represents one topic, and words listed after the number are 5 keywords of that topic.

    Please read the following example.

  • Context Sentence: Google announces a new product.
  • Provided topics:
    • 0, development, design, innovation, designing, developing
    • 1, Google, Facebook, Apple, Amazon, Microsoft
    • 2, products, product, merchandise, commodity, commerce
    • 3, just, actually, really, going, still
    • 4, retailer, retailers, selling, brands, retail
    • 5, 2011, 2010, 2012, 2009, 2008
    • 6, Obama, Biden, Sanders, Bush, Trump
    • 7, announce, announces, declare, declared, claim
    • 8, investment, investments, profitability, equity, financial
    • 9, Software, Desktop, Apps, Networking, Windows
  • a) Topics that do NOT promote the narrative (i.e., the topics that do not provide additional information if being observed in the continuation)
    • 2, products, product, merchandise, commodity, commerce (the context sentence has mentioned the topic)
    • 3, just, actually, really, going, still (the topic itself does not have a clear meaning)
    • 7, announce, announces, declare, declared, claim (the context sentence has mentioned the topic)
  • b) Topics that are NOT very likely to appear in reasonable continuations (i.e., it is hard to imagine a reasonable continuation that mentions the topic or the topic does not have a clear meaning)
    • 3, just, actually, really, going, still (the topic itself does not have a clear meaning)
    • 6, Obama, Biden, Sanders, Bush, Trump (not relevant to this context sentence)
    • 7, announce, announces, declare, declared, claim (the context sentence has mentioned the topic and the topics are not very likely to be seen in the continuation)

    What if the meaning of the context sentence is hard to understand? If you encounter such a sentence, please still try to answer the questions by guessing the meaning of the context sentence and type "Meaning is unclear" in the textbox for additional comments.

    What do reasonable continuations mean? The task is to guess the topics the author of the context sentence might say next in Wikipedia based on only the context sentence. What actually happens in the real world is not important. What actually is written in Wikipedia is also not important. You do not need to spend too much time guessing what exactly the author will say, but your guess should not be unlikely (e.g., imagine funny continuations when the context sentence is serious or imagine something that violates logic or commonsense).

    Task Instructions for Section 2 (Click to expand)

    In Section 2, You will be given a continuation of the context sentence. The continuation is automatically generated by a machine.

    Your job is to evaluate

  • a) how fluent the generated sentence is,
  • b) how much this generated continuation promotes the narrative, and
  • c) what overall quality the generated continuation is.

    • Notice that the final sentence/word generated by the machine might be incomplete and your job is only judging the fluency of the generated continuation. The fluency includes whether the transition from the context sentence to the generated text is smooth, but you should ignore the fluency of the context sentence itself, ignore whether the final sentence/word is complete, and ignore extra spaces when doing the task.

    To make the fluency judgement easier, we repeat the context sentence in Section 2.

    Please read the following examples.

    Example 1:

  • Context Sentence: I love love cat and dog.
  • Generated Continuation: I also love to go jogging in the mor
  • a) How fluent is the generated continuation: Very fluent (because the not fluent part is in the context sentence itself. It is fine if the last sentence is not finished or the last word is not completed.)
  • b) How helpful this generated continuation promote the narrative: Helpful
  • c) Overall, how good the generated continuation is: Excellent (the continuation is like what humans will write)

    • Example 2:

  • Context Sentence: I love a dog.
  • Generated Continuation: I really love this dog.
  • a) How fluent is the generated continuation: Very fluent
  • b) How helpful this generated continuation promotes the narrative: Not helpful
  • c) Overall, how good the generated continuation is: Medium (the continuation is understandable but contains some obvious flaws or repetitions)

    • Example 3:

  • Context Sentence: I love a dog.
  • Generated Continuation: I love a dog.
  • a) How fluent is the generated continuation: Not fluent (the generated text is a copy of the context sentence, so the transition is not good.)
  • b) How helpful this generated continuation promote the narrative: Not helpful at all
  • c) Overall, how good the generated continuation is: Bad (it is hard to understand the continuation due to some severe flaws or repetitions)

    • Example 4:

  • Context Sentence: I love a dog.
  • Generated Continuation: yes, but no I can
  • a) How fluent is the generated continuation: Not fluent at all
  • b) How helpful this generated continuation promote the narrative: Not helpful
  • c) Overall, how good the generated continuation is: Terrible (the continuation does not make any sense)

    • You should finish Section 1 before seeing the generated continuation in Section 2.
